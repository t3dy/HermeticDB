<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">كتاب سر الأسرار / Secretum Secretorum</p>
<p>The Secretum Secretorum (Secret of Secrets) is a pseudo-Aristotelian compendium framed as a series of letters from Aristotle to Alexander the Great, covering statecraft, morality, physiognomy, medicine, astrology, and alchemy. The original Arabic text, the Kitab Sirr al-Asrar, was likely composed in the ninth or tenth century CE, drawing on earlier Persian and Greek advisory literature. It became one of the most widely read books of the European High Middle Ages.</p><p>The work integrates the Emerald Tablet into its alchemical sections, presenting the Tablet's axioms as wisdom imparted within the framework of Aristotelian political and natural philosophy. This embedding gave the Tablet a distinctive reception context, linking alchemical knowledge to royal authority and practical governance. The compendium's enormous popularity ensured that the Emerald Tablet reached a far wider audience through this channel than through specialized alchemical manuscripts alone.</p><p>The Secretum Secretorum was first translated into Latin in an abridged form by John of Seville around 1140, and then fully by Philip of Tripoli around 1243. The Latin versions circulated in hundreds of manuscripts across medieval Europe, making it one of the most transmitted texts of the period. Roger Bacon produced an annotated edition, further enhancing its scholarly prestige.</p><p>For the study of Hermeticism, the Secretum Secretorum is essential as a vehicle through which Hermetic alchemical ideas penetrated mainstream medieval culture. Its pseudo-Aristotelian framing legitimized alchemical and Hermetic content within Scholastic intellectual circles that might otherwise have been hostile to such material.</p>
<h3 style="margin-top:1.5rem;font-size:1.1rem">Transmission</h3>
<p>First translated to Latin by John of Seville c. 1140 (abridged), then fully by Philip of Tripoli c. 1243.</p>
