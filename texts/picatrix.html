Picatrix (Ghayat al-Hakim)

Unknown Era · ARABIC

ARABIC · COMPILATION · 950–1000 CE

غاية الحكيم

The Picatrix is the Latin title of the Ghayat al-Hakim (The Aim of the Sage), a comprehensive Arabic treatise on talismanic magic, celestial invocation, and Hermetic-Neoplatonic cosmology. The Arabic original was composed in the mid-tenth to eleventh century CE, likely in al-Andalus, and was translated into Castilian in 1256 at the court of Alfonso X of Castile, with a Latin translation following shortly thereafter.

The work synthesizes an enormous range of sources into a systematic manual of astrological magic, covering the theory and practice of creating talismans, invoking planetary spirits, and harnessing celestial influences for terrestrial purposes. It draws heavily on Hermetic authority, citing Hermes Trismegistus as a foundational figure and incorporating doctrines from both the philosophical and operative Hermetic traditions. The text also transmits material from Sabian religious practices, Neoplatonic metaphysics, and Indian astrological traditions.

The Picatrix served as a major conduit for the transmission of Hermetic magical practice from the Islamic world into medieval and Renaissance Europe. Its Latin translation was widely read, though often clandestinely, and influenced figures including Marsilio Ficino and Cornelius Agrippa. The work demonstrates the operative dimension of Hermeticism at its most systematic, providing detailed instructions for ritual practices grounded in a coherent cosmological framework.

For the study of Hermeticism, the Picatrix is essential as evidence that the Hermetic tradition encompassed not only philosophical theology and alchemical theory but also a sophisticated tradition of astral magic that was transmitted, adapted, and contested across linguistic and cultural boundaries.
