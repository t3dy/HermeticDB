<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Liber super textum Hermetis</p>
<p>The Hortulanus Commentary (Liber super textum Hermetis) is the most widely disseminated medieval Latin exegesis of the Emerald Tablet, composed by the otherwise little-known author Hortulanus (the Gardener), probably in the fourteenth century. The commentary takes the vulgate Latin text of the Emerald Tablet as its base and provides a verse-by-verse interpretation.</p><p>Hortulanus reads the Tablet as a sequential laboratory manual for creating the Philosopher's Stone. He decodes the Tablet's celestial imagery, interpreting sun and moon as sulfur and mercury according to the prevailing alchemical theory, and reads the process of separation described in the text as mundification (purification) through the application of gentle heat. This practical, operational reading anchored the Emerald Tablet firmly within the tradition of laboratory alchemy, distinguishing Hortulanus's approach from more philosophical or mystical interpretations.</p><p>The commentary achieved extraordinary circulation in the medieval and early modern periods, becoming the standard framework through which European readers encountered and understood the Emerald Tablet. Its widespread manuscript transmission and later inclusion in printed alchemical anthologies ensured that the Hortulanus interpretation shaped the reception of the Tablet for centuries. The commentary is essential for understanding how the Emerald Tablet was transformed from an enigmatic cosmological text into a practical alchemical guide in the Latin West.</p>
<h3 style="margin-top:1.5rem;font-size:1.1rem">Transmission</h3>
<p>Most widely disseminated commentary. Anchored the Tablet within practical laboratory chemistry.</p>
