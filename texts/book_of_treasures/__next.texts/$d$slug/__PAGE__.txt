<p>The Book of Treasures (Kitab al-Dhakha'ir) is a Syriac encyclopedic compilation composed by Job of Edessa (Ayyub al-Ruhawi), who died around 835 CE. Written in Syriac, a major literary and scholarly language of the late antique and early medieval Near East, the work covers topics in natural philosophy and cosmology.</p><p>The Book of Treasures is significant for the study of Hermeticism primarily because of its conceptual similarities with the Arabic Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa (Book of the Secret of Creation), the text that contains the earliest known embedding of the Emerald Tablet. Scholars including Ruska, Plessner, and Kraus have identified a shared problemataphysica source between Job's Syriac compilation and the Sirr al-Khaliqa, suggesting that both works drew upon common late antique Syriac intellectual traditions.</p><p>While the Book of Treasures does not itself contain the Emerald Tablet, its textual and conceptual parallels with the Sirr al-Khaliqa provide crucial evidence for reconstructing the intermediary transmission networks through which Greek Neoplatonic and Hermetic ideas passed into the Arabic alchemical tradition. The work thus serves as an important witness to the Syriac milieu that likely played a key role in shaping the Emerald Tablet's earliest literary context.</p>
