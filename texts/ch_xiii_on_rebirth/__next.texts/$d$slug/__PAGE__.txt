<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Περὶ Παλιγγενεσίας</p>
<p>Corpus Hermeticum XIII, known as On Rebirth or On Regeneration (Peri Palingenesias), is a Greek Hermetic treatise in dialogue form in which Hermes Trismegistus instructs his son and disciple Tat on the process of spiritual regeneration. The text was composed in the Greco-Egyptian milieu, likely between the second and third centuries CE, as part of the broader Hermetic philosophical literature.</p><p>The treatise describes an initiatory process in which the aspirant must shed the twelve torments of material identification, replacing them with ten divine powers, in order to achieve a rebirth of consciousness. This transformation is presented not as a physical event but as an interior experience of becoming divine, dissolving the false self constituted by bodily passions and assuming the mind of God. The text occupies an ambiguous position between mystery-religion initiation and philosophical gnosis, blending the language of ritual transformation with Platonic and Stoic psychological categories.</p><p>On Rebirth is significant for understanding the experiential and soteriological dimensions of Hermeticism. It demonstrates that the Hermetic tradition encompassed not only cosmological speculation and natural philosophy but also a concrete program of spiritual transformation aimed at deification. Renaissance readers, particularly Ficino and later Khunrath, drew on this treatise's account of spiritual rebirth in constructing their own theosophical programs.</p>
