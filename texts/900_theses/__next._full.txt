<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Conclusiones philosophicae, cabalisticae et theologicae</p>
<p>The 900 Theses (Conclusiones philosophicae, cabalisticae et theologicae) is a collection of nine hundred philosophical propositions composed in Latin by Giovanni Pico della Mirandola and published in Rome in 1486. The work was intended as the basis for a public disputation before Pope Innocent VIII and leading scholars, though the debate was ultimately suppressed by papal authority after thirteen of the theses were condemned as heretical.</p><p>The Conclusiones represent one of the most ambitious intellectual syntheses of the Renaissance, drawing together Hermetic, Kabbalistic, Neoplatonic, Aristotelian, Chaldean, and Christian doctrines into a unified philosophical framework. Ten of the theses refer directly to Hermes Trismegistus, asserting principles drawn from the Corpus Hermeticum and the Asclepius concerning the hierarchy of soul, mind, and God, the omnipresence of life in the cosmos, and the impossibility of true destruction.</p><p>Pico's work radicalized the Hermetic synthesis that Marsilio Ficino had initiated through his 1463 translation of the Corpus Hermeticum. Where Ficino positioned Hermes as a forerunner of Plato, Pico went further, integrating Hermetic wisdom with Jewish Kabbalah and proposing a universal concordance of religious and philosophical traditions. His introduction of Christian Cabala to the Latin West, presented alongside Hermetic theses, created a new paradigm for understanding ancient wisdom as a convergent whole.</p><p>The 900 Theses remain essential for understanding how Hermetic ideas were absorbed, transformed, and defended within Renaissance intellectual culture. They mark a pivotal moment when Hermeticism moved from a private scholarly interest into a public theological controversy.</p>
