Corpus Hermeticum

Unknown Era · GREEK

GREEK · PRIMARY_SOURCE · 100–300 CE

The Corpus Hermeticum is a collection of eighteen Greek philosophical and theological treatises attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, the legendary sage identified with the Egyptian god Thoth. The individual treatises were composed between approximately the first and third centuries CE in the Greco-Egyptian milieu, though the collection as we know it was likely assembled around the fourth century CE.

The treatises address fundamental questions of cosmogony, theology, soteriology, and the nature of divine mind (Nous). They present a distinctive spiritual philosophy in which the human soul, through knowledge (gnosis) of its divine origin, can ascend through the cosmic spheres to reunion with the supreme God. The collection opens with the Poimandres (CH I), a foundational cosmogonic vision, and includes treatises on rebirth (CH XIII), the properties of God, and the relationship between mind, soul, and matter. The philosophical orientation synthesizes Platonic, Stoic, and Egyptian religious elements.

The Corpus Hermeticum's historical impact was transformative. Brought to Florence from Macedonia around 1460, it was translated into Latin by Marsilio Ficino in 1463 at the request of Cosimo de' Medici, who reportedly prioritized it over Plato's dialogues. Ficino's translation, published as the Pimander, ignited the Renaissance Hermetic revival and established Hermes Trismegistus as a prisca theologia authority. Isaac Casaubon's 1614 redating of the texts to the post-Christian era challenged but did not eliminate their philosophical influence.

The Corpus Hermeticum remains the foundational text of the philosophical Hermetic tradition and is essential for understanding the interplay between Egyptian religion, Greek philosophy, and the Western esoteric tradition from antiquity through the Renaissance and beyond.
