<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Asclepius</p>
<p>The Asclepius is a Latin Hermetic dialogue attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, surviving as the only substantial Hermetic philosophical text transmitted in Latin from late antiquity. The original Greek version, known as the Logos Teleios (Perfect Discourse), is lost, though fragments survive in other sources and a Coptic version was found at Nag Hammadi. The Latin translation has traditionally been attributed to Apuleius of Madaura, though this attribution is debated.</p><p>The text takes the form of a dialogue between Hermes and his disciple Asclepius, covering topics of cosmology, theology, and ritual practice. Its most distinctive passages address the animation of cult statues through the art of drawing celestial spirits into material images (sections 38-41), a practice that bridges philosophical and operative Hermeticism. Equally famous is the apocalyptic prophecy of Egypt's divine abandonment (sections 24-26), the so-called Lament, which foretells a time when the gods will desert Egypt and its sacred traditions will perish.</p><p>The Asclepius played a central role in the medieval and Renaissance reception of Hermeticism. Church Fathers including Augustine cited the statue-animation passage as evidence of demonic magic, ensuring the text remained controversial yet widely known throughout the Middle Ages. The immaterial thanksgiving prayer (section 41) influenced both Christian liturgical thought and Renaissance discussions of natural magic. Scholastic and humanist thinkers from Guillaume d'Auvergne to Marsilio Ficino engaged with the text.</p><p>As a bridge between the philosophical and operative dimensions of Hermeticism, the Asclepius is essential for understanding the full range of the Hermetic tradition and its contested reception across Christian, Islamic, and Renaissance cultures.</p>
