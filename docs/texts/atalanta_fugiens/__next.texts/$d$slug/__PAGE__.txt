<p>The Atalanta Fugiens (Atalanta Fleeing) is a Latin emblem book composed by the German physician and alchemist Michael Maier (1568-1622) and published in 1618 at Oppenheim by Johann Theodor de Bry. It is one of the most sophisticated multimedia works of the early modern period, combining fifty alchemical emblems with accompanying epigrams, discourses, and musical fugues for three voices.</p><p>Each emblem consists of a copper-engraved image, a motto, an explanatory epigram, and a musical composition, creating a synesthetic experience that engages sight, intellect, and hearing simultaneously. Emblem I is of particular significance for Hermetic studies, depicting the axiom from the Emerald Tablet that the wind carried the philosophical subject in its womb. The work systematically encodes alchemical processes and principles through mythological narratives drawn from classical antiquity.</p><p>The Atalanta Fugiens belongs to the broader Renaissance emblematic tradition but stands apart in its depth of engagement with Hermetic philosophy and its innovative use of music as a vehicle for alchemical meaning. Maier, who served as physician to Emperor Rudolf II, was deeply immersed in the Hermetic and Paracelsian currents of his time, and the work reflects his conviction that alchemical truth could be communicated through symbolic and artistic means as well as through discursive argument.</p><p>The work remains a landmark in the visual and intellectual history of alchemy and Hermeticism, demonstrating how the Emerald Tablet's axioms were woven into the fabric of early modern European learned culture through creative reinterpretation.</p>
