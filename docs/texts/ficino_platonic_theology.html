Platonic Theology (Theologia Platonica)

Unknown Era · LATIN

LATIN · TREATISE · 1469–1474 CE

Theologia Platonica de immortalitate animae

The Theologia Platonica de immortalitate animorum (Platonic Theology on the Immortality of Souls) is a major Latin philosophical work composed by Marsilio Ficino (1433-1499) and published in 1482. It represents Ficino's most ambitious attempt to synthesize Neoplatonic philosophy with Christian theology, arguing for the immortality and divinity of the human soul.

Ficino positions Hermes Trismegistus as a key figure in the prisca theologia (ancient theology), a lineage of divinely inspired sages stretching from remote antiquity to Plato and ultimately anticipating Christian revelation. In this framework, the Hermetic writings serve as evidence that pagan philosophical wisdom converges with and foreshadows Christian truth. Ficino draws on both the Corpus Hermeticum, which he had translated into Latin in 1463, and the Asclepius in constructing his argument.

The Platonic Theology is significant for the history of Hermeticism because it provided the most influential philosophical framework within which Hermetic ideas were received and legitimated in Renaissance Europe. By integrating Hermes into the prisca theologia alongside Moses, Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Plato, Ficino ensured that Hermetic texts would be read not as marginal curiosities but as central documents of a perennial wisdom tradition. This interpretive framework shaped the reception of Hermeticism well into the seventeenth century.
