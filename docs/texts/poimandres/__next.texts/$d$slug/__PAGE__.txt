<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Ποιμάνδρης</p>
<p>The Poimandres is the opening treatise of the Corpus Hermeticum (CH I), composed in Greek in the Greco-Egyptian cultural milieu, likely during the second or third century CE. The title derives from the name of the divine figure who appears to the narrator: Poimandres, identified as the Divine Mind or Nous.</p><p>The text presents a cosmogonic vision in which Poimandres reveals the creation of the cosmos and the descent of the Anthropos (Primal Human) into the material world. The narrative describes how the divine Anthropos, originally a being of pure light, falls in love with Nature upon seeing his reflection in the waters below, and thereby becomes entangled in matter. The treatise then outlines the path of ascent by which the soul can shed its material accretions and return to the divine realm, passing through the seven planetary spheres. The cosmogony synthesizes elements of Genesis, Platonic metaphysics, Stoic physics, and Egyptian religious thought.</p><p>The Poimandres was the first Hermetic text translated by Marsilio Ficino in 1463, and its cosmogonic vision became the foundation for Renaissance understandings of Hermeticism. The text's account of the divine Anthropos and the fall into matter resonated with both Christian and Neoplatonic readers, while its description of spiritual ascent influenced theurgical and mystical traditions from Iamblichus through Khunrath.</p><p>As the programmatic opening of the Corpus Hermeticum, the Poimandres establishes the fundamental metaphysical framework of the philosophical Hermetic tradition and remains the single most studied and commented-upon Hermetic text.</p>
