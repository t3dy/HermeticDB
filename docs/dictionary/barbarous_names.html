Barbarous Names (Onomata Barbara)

LINGUISTIC

Further Extracted Evidence

  • A major thread of Fraser’s argument is that Iamblichus’ defense of barbarous names (foreign, ineffable invocations) parallels Hermetic writings—especially Corpus Hermeticum 16. [eref]
  • KYLE FRASER 397 ————— Greek, Roman, and Byzantine Studies 65 (2025) 386–409 In his extended treatment of Egyptian theology in Book 7, Iamblichus discusses the theurgic significance of such so-called barbarous names (ὀνόµατα βάρβαρα), which he divides into two kinds: the ineffable names (ἄσηµα ὀνόµατα), which are nonsensi- cal in human terms, and the traditional names or epithets of the gods in foreign languages, chiefly Assyrian42 and Egyptian. [eref]
  • This Hermetic assessment of the superiority of Egyptian as a ritual language finds a striking parallel in the arguments of Iamblichus in defence of the theurgic use of barbarous names. [eref]
  • In the eyes of Porphyry, what Iamblichus called theurgy was just a loftier name for ‘magic’—the barbarous names of theur- gists are the very same devices by which lowly magicians attempt to bind the gods to their will.69 Magic, as Porphyry understands it, is essentially coercive and it is premised on bad theology. [eref]
