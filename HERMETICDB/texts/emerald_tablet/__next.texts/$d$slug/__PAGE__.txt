<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">اللوح الزمردي / Tabula Smaragdina</p>
<p>The Emerald Tablet (Tabula Smaragdina) is a compact, cryptic text attributed to Hermes Trismegistus that became the seminal theoretical document of both Western and Islamicate alchemical traditions. Its earliest known appearance is within the Arabic Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa (Book of the Secret of Creation), attributed to Balinas (Apollonius of Tyana), likely composed between the sixth and eighth centuries CE. The original language of the Tablet is Arabic, though claims of deeper antiquity have persisted throughout its reception history.</p><p>The text consists of a series of terse, enigmatic axioms encoding a synthesis of Aristotelian natural physics and Neoplatonic emanationist cosmology. Its most famous dictum, asserting the correspondence between what is above and what is below, articulates the macrocosm-microcosm principle that became foundational to alchemical theory. The Tablet describes a single creative process by which a universal substance descends from heaven, is nurtured by earth, and accomplishes the transmutation of all things, a formula read by commentators as both a cosmological statement and a practical guide to the creation of the Philosopher's Stone.</p><p>The Emerald Tablet entered Latin Europe through multiple twelfth-century translation vectors, including Hugo of Santalla's De secretis naturae (preserving the full frame narrative), the vulgate Latin version likely by Plato of Tivoli (a standalone aphoristic text), and the pseudo-Aristotelian Secretum Secretorum. Major medieval commentaries by Hortulanus, Albertus Magnus, and Roger Bacon cemented the Tablet's authority.</p><p>The Emerald Tablet is arguably the single most influential short text in the Western esoteric tradition, shaping alchemical thought from the medieval Arabic world through the Renaissance and into early modern chemistry and theosophy.</p>
<h3 style="margin-top:1.5rem;font-size:1.1rem">Transmission</h3>
<p>Entered Latin Europe through Arabic science via multiple 12th-century translation vectors. Circulated both embedded in larger works and as a standalone text.</p>
