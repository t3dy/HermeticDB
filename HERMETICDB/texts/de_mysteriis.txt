<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Περὶ τῶν μυστηρίων</p>
<p>De mysteriis Aegyptiorum (On the Mysteries of the Egyptians), composed in Greek by the Neoplatonic philosopher Iamblichus of Chalcis (ca. 245-325 CE), is a systematic defense of theurgy against the rationalist critique of his teacher Porphyry. Written under the persona of the Egyptian priest Abamon, the work argues that ritual practice, not philosophical reasoning alone, is the proper path to divine union.</p><p>Iamblichus treats Hermetic texts as genuine translations of the Egyptian Books of Thoth, granting them a privileged epistemological status as repositories of ancient priestly wisdom. A central argument of the work is that ritual language and divine names carry inherent sacred energy that fades through translation, a concept he terms exitalos. This doctrine has profound implications for understanding how Hermetic texts were valued not merely for their philosophical content but for their perceived ritual efficacy rooted in their Egyptian origins.</p><p>De mysteriis occupies a pivotal position in the history of Hermeticism as the key text linking the Hermetic and Neoplatonic traditions. Iamblichus's incorporation of Hermetic authority into his theurgical framework ensured that Hermeticism would be understood not only as a philosophical system but as a hieratic tradition grounded in ritual practice. His work influenced Proclus and, through Renaissance reception, shaped Ficino's and Pico's understanding of Hermetic magic.</p><p>The treatise is essential for understanding the operative and ritualistic dimensions of Hermeticism that complement the more philosophical orientation of the Corpus Hermeticum, and for tracing the genealogy of Western ceremonial magic back to late antique Neoplatonic theurgy.</p>
