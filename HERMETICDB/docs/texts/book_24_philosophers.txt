<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Liber XXIV Philosophorum</p>
<p>The Liber XXIV Philosophorum (Book of the Twenty-Four Philosophers) is a short Latin text consisting of twenty-four aphoristic definitions of God, each attributed to a different unnamed philosopher. Though its origins are debated, the text likely derives from a late antique Greek or Latin source, with the earliest known manuscripts dating to the twelfth century.</p><p>The most celebrated of its definitions is the formulation that God is an infinite sphere whose center is everywhere and whose circumference is nowhere, a paradoxical image that profoundly influenced medieval and early modern philosophical theology. The definitions range across Neoplatonic, Hermetic, and pre-Scholastic conceptual territory, employing compressed philosophical language to articulate the nature of divine unity, infinity, and self-knowledge.</p><p>The Liber XXIV Philosophorum circulated widely in medieval Europe and was studied by major thinkers including Alan of Lille, Meister Eckhart, and Nicholas of Cusa. The text was often associated with Hermetic literature, and its aphoristic style and theological content align it with the tradition of Hermetic wisdom sayings.
