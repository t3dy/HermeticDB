Three Books of Occult Philosophy (De occulta philosophia)

Unknown Era · LATIN

LATIN · TREATISE · 1510–1533 CE

De occulta philosophia libri tres

De occulta philosophia libri tres (Three Books of Occult Philosophy) is a comprehensive Latin treatise on magic composed by Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa von Nettesheim (1486-1535). The work was first circulated in manuscript around 1510, revised and expanded over the following two decades, and published in its final form in 1533. It is the most influential systematic treatise on magic produced in the early modern period.

The three books are organized according to a threefold division of the cosmos: natural magic (the elemental world), celestial magic (the astral world), and ceremonial or intellectual magic (the supercelestial world). Agrippa synthesizes Hermetic, Neoplatonic, Kabbalistic, Pythagorean, and astrological traditions into a unified theoretical framework, drawing heavily on the Corpus Hermeticum, the Asclepius, the Picatrix, and the works of Ficino and Pico della Mirandola. The work also incorporates material from Reuchlin's Christian Cabala and Trithemius's magical theology.

Agrippa's treatise provided the foundational theoretical architecture for virtually all subsequent Western ceremonial magic. By systematizing disparate Hermetic, Kabbalistic, and Neoplatonic sources into a coherent practical framework, Agrippa made these traditions accessible to a broad educated audience. His work was a key reference for later figures including John Dee, Giordano Bruno, and Heinrich Khunrath.

For the history of Hermeticism, the Three Books of Occult Philosophy represent the moment when Hermetic ideas were most fully integrated into a practical magical system, demonstrating the operative potential of Hermetic cosmology and its enduring influence on Western esoteric traditions.
