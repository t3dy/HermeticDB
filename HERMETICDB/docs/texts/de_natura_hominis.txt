<p style="font-style:italic;color:#6b5d4d">Περὶ φύσεως ἀνθρώπου</p>
<p>De Natura Hominis (On the Nature of Man) is a Greek philosophical treatise composed by Nemesius, Bishop of Emesa in Syria, around 400 CE. The work presents a comprehensive anthropology defining the human being as a microcosm that binds together the celestial and terrestrial realms, drawing extensively on Middle Platonic, Stoic, and Aristotelian philosophical sources.</p><p>Nemesius's treatise is one of the first systematic attempts to articulate a holistic philosophical anthropology in the Christian world, synthesizing pagan philosophical traditions with theological concerns. Its central doctrine of macrocosm-microcosm correspondence, in which the human being recapitulates the structure of the entire cosmos, represents the direct theoretical precursor to the Emerald Tablet's most famous axiom concerning the relationship between what is above and what is below.</p><p>The text's connection to the Hermetic tradition is not merely conceptual but textual. An Arabic translation of material from De Natura Hominis appears at the end of the longer recension of the Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa, demonstrating that the compilers of the Sirr were actively drawing on Nemesius's Neoplatonic anthropology in constructing their alchemical-cosmological synthesis. Scholars including Kraus and Plessner established this connection, which provides crucial evidence for dating and contextualizing the composition of the Sirr al-Khaliqa.</p><p>De Natura Hominis is essential for understanding the intellectual sources behind the Emerald Tablet and for tracing the transmission of macrocosm-microcosm doctrine from Greek philosophy through Syriac intermediaries into the Arabic alchemical tradition.</p>
<h3 style="margin-top:1.5rem;font-size:1.1rem">Transmission</h3>
<p>An Arabic translation appears at the end of the Sirr al-Khaliqa, proving the compilers were actively synthesizing Nemesius's Neoplatonic anthropology with alchemical theory.</p>
